Exam objective 2.1

Compare and contrast common threat actors and motivations

Threat actors are individuals, groups, or entities that pose security threats to organizations. Understanding different types of threat actors, their capabilities, and motivations is essential for developing appropriate security measures and incident response strategies.

Threat Actors

Nation-state Nation-state actors are government-sponsored groups that conduct cyber operations to advance national interests. They typically have significant resources, advanced capabilities, and long-term strategic objectives.

Characteristics:

  • Extensive financial and technical resources
  • Access to zero-day exploits and advanced tools
  • Highly skilled personnel with specialized training
  • Long-term persistence and patience
  • Focus on strategic intelligence gathering

Common targets:

  • Government agencies and military organizations
  • Critical infrastructure (power grids, water systems)
  • Defense contractors and aerospace companies
  • Telecommunications and technology companies
  • Financial institutions and economic data

Examples of nation-state activities:

  • Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) for long-term espionage
  • Cyber warfare operations against adversary nations
  • Economic espionage to gain competitive advantages
  • Disruption of critical infrastructure during conflicts
  • Influence operations and disinformation campaigns

Unskilled Attacker (Script Kiddie) Unskilled attackers are individuals with limited technical knowledge who use existing tools and scripts created by others to conduct attacks. They typically lack the expertise to develop their own exploits.

Characteristics:

  • Limited technical skills and understanding
  • Rely on automated tools and pre-written scripts
  • Often motivated by curiosity or desire for recognition
  • Opportunistic rather than targeted in their approach
  • May cause significant damage despite limited skills

Common activities:

  • Using readily available hacking tools and exploits
  • Defacing websites for recognition or bragging rights
  • Launching denial-of-service attacks using botnets
  • Attempting to gain unauthorized access to systems
  • Participating in online hacking communities

Hacktivist Hacktivists are individuals or groups who use hacking techniques to promote political or social causes. They combine hacking skills with activism to raise awareness or protest against perceived injustices.

Characteristics:

  • Motivated by political, social, or ideological beliefs
  • Often work in loosely organized groups or collectives
  • Use cyber attacks as a form of digital protest
  • May have varying levels of technical sophistication
  • Often seek media attention for their causes

Common activities:

  • Website defacements with political messages
  • Distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks against targets
  • Data breaches to expose perceived wrongdoing
  • Leaking sensitive information to the public
  • Disrupting services of organizations they oppose

Examples of hacktivist groups:

  • Anonymous - decentralized collective known for various operations
  • LulzSec - group that conducted high-profile attacks for entertainment
  • Syrian Electronic Army - pro-Syrian government hacktivist group

Insider Threat Insider threats come from individuals within an organization who have authorized access to systems and data but use that access maliciously or negligently.

Types of insider threats:

  • Malicious insiders who intentionally cause harm
  • Negligent insiders who accidentally cause security incidents
  • Compromised insiders whose credentials have been stolen

Characteristics:

  • Legitimate access to organizational systems and data
  • Knowledge of internal processes and security measures
  • Ability to bypass many security controls
  • Difficult to detect using traditional security tools
  • Can cause significant damage due to trusted access

Risk factors:

  • Financial difficulties or personal problems
  • Dissatisfaction with employment or management
  • Ideological conflicts with organizational policies
  • Lack of security awareness or training
  • Inadequate access controls and monitoring

Organized Crime Organized crime groups conduct cyber attacks primarily for financial gain. They operate sophisticated criminal enterprises with clear hierarchies and specialized roles.

Characteristics:

  • Profit-driven motivation and business-like operations
  • Sophisticated tools and techniques
  • International scope and coordination
  • Specialization in different types of cybercrime
  • Continuous evolution to avoid law enforcement

Common activities:

  • Ransomware attacks for financial extortion
  • Credit card fraud and identity theft
  • Banking trojans and financial malware
  • Cryptocurrency theft and money laundering
  • Human trafficking and illegal marketplaces

Shadow IT Shadow IT refers to information technology systems, devices, software, applications, and services used within organizations without explicit organizational approval or oversight.

Characteristics:

  • Unauthorized use of technology resources
  • Often well-intentioned but creates security risks
  • Bypasses established security controls and policies
  • May introduce vulnerabilities and compliance issues
  • Difficult to monitor and manage

Common examples:

  • Employees using personal cloud storage services
  • Installing unauthorized software applications
  • Using personal devices for business purposes
  • Setting up unauthorized network connections
  • Implementing unsanctioned business solutions

Attributes of Actors

Internal/External This attribute distinguishes between threats originating from within the organization versus those from outside sources.

Internal threats:

  • Employees, contractors, and business partners
  • Have legitimate access to organizational resources
  • Familiar with internal processes and security measures
  • Can be malicious, negligent, or compromised
  • Often more difficult to detect and prevent

External threats:

  • Attackers operating from outside the organization
  • Must find ways to gain initial access to systems
  • May use social engineering or technical exploits
  • Can be identified and blocked more easily
  • Often leave more obvious traces of their activities

Resources/Funding The level of resources available to threat actors significantly impacts their capabilities and the sophistication of their attacks.

High-resource actors (nation-states, organized crime):

  • Access to advanced tools and zero-day exploits
  • Ability to conduct long-term, persistent campaigns
  • Skilled personnel and specialized expertise
  • Financial resources for research and development
  • Capability to target hardened, high-value systems

Low-resource actors (script kiddies, individual hackers):

  • Limited to freely available tools and techniques
  • Focus on opportunistic, easy targets
  • May rely on social engineering rather than technical exploits
  • Limited ability to sustain long-term operations
  • Often target systems with known vulnerabilities

Level of Sophistication/Capability This attribute describes the technical skills and advanced techniques available to different threat actors.

Advanced capabilities:

  • Custom malware development and zero-day exploits
  • Advanced persistent threat (APT) techniques
  • Sophisticated social engineering and reconnaissance
  • Ability to evade detection and maintain persistence
  • Multi-stage attacks with complex objectives

Basic capabilities:

  • Use of existing tools and known exploits
  • Simple attack techniques and methodologies
  • Limited ability to adapt to defensive measures
  • Focus on targets of opportunity
  • Reliance on automated tools and scripts

Motivations

Understanding threat actor motivations helps organizations predict likely attack vectors and implement appropriate defensive measures.

Data Exfiltration The theft of sensitive information from organizational systems for various purposes including espionage, financial gain, or competitive advantage.

Common targets:

  • Intellectual property and trade secrets
  • Customer databases and personal information
  • Financial records and payment card data
  • Government secrets and classified information
  • Healthcare records and research data

Espionage The practice of obtaining secret or confidential information for political, military, or economic advantage.

Types of espionage:

  • Corporate espionage for competitive advantage
  • Political espionage for diplomatic intelligence
  • Military espionage for defense information
  • Economic espionage for trade advantages
  • Cyber espionage using digital techniques

Service Disruption Attacks designed to make services unavailable to legitimate users, often through denial-of-service attacks or system destruction.

Impact of service disruption:

  • Loss of revenue and business operations
  • Damage to reputation and customer trust
  • Regulatory penalties and legal consequences
  • Recovery costs and system restoration
  • Potential safety risks in critical systems

Blackmail The practice of demanding payment or other concessions under threat of revealing embarrassing or damaging information.

Common blackmail scenarios:

  • Ransomware attacks encrypting critical data
  • Threatening to release sensitive information
  • Demanding payment to prevent service disruption
  • Extorting individuals with compromising information
  • Corporate extortion using stolen trade secrets

Financial Gain Direct monetary profit from cyber attacks through various means including theft, fraud, and extortion.

Methods of financial gain:

  • Credit card fraud and identity theft
  • Banking trojans and account takeovers
  • Cryptocurrency theft and mining
  • Ransomware and extortion schemes
  • Selling stolen data on dark markets

Philosophical/Political Beliefs Attacks motivated by ideological convictions, political activism, or desire to promote specific causes or beliefs.

Examples of ideologically motivated attacks:

  • Hacktivism promoting social or political causes
  • Attacks against organizations with opposing views
  • Disruption of government services or policies
  • Promoting freedom of information and transparency
  • Protesting corporate or government actions

Ethical Attacks conducted by individuals who believe they are exposing wrongdoing or promoting greater good, often called "ethical hackers" or "white hat" hackers.

Ethical hacking activities:

  • Responsible disclosure of security vulnerabilities
  • Penetration testing with proper authorization
  • Security research to improve defensive measures
  • Whistleblowing to expose organizational misconduct
  • Educational activities to raise security awareness

Revenge Attacks motivated by personal grievances, perceived injustices, or desire for retaliation against specific individuals or organizations.

Common revenge scenarios:

  • Disgruntled employees attacking former employers
  • Personal disputes leading to cyber harassment
  • Retaliation for perceived slights or injustices
  • Attacks following termination or disciplinary action
  • Personal vendettas using cyber capabilities

Disruption/Chaos Attacks aimed at causing maximum disruption, confusion, or chaos without specific strategic objectives beyond creating mayhem.

Characteristics of chaos-motivated attacks:

  • Random or opportunistic target selection
  • Focus on maximum impact and visibility
  • Often conducted for entertainment or notoriety
  • May involve multiple attack vectors simultaneously
  • Difficult to predict or defend against

War Cyber attacks conducted as part of military operations or conflicts between nations, often targeting critical infrastructure and military capabilities.

Cyber warfare characteristics:

  • State-sponsored attacks against adversary nations
  • Targeting of critical infrastructure and military systems
  • Coordination with conventional military operations
  • Long-term strategic objectives and planning
  • Potential for escalation to physical conflicts

🏛️ Nation-State Actors

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs):

  • Resources: Government funding, advanced tools, zero-days
  • Capabilities: Custom malware, supply chain attacks, long-term campaigns
  • Targets: Critical infrastructure, government agencies, defense contractors
  • Motivations: Espionage, intellectual property theft, geopolitical advantage
  • Examples: APT1, Lazarus Group, Fancy Bear, Cozy Bear

👶 Script Kiddies

Low-skill opportunistic attackers:

  • Resources: Limited funding, publicly available tools
  • Capabilities: Use existing exploits, basic social engineering
  • Targets: Opportunistic, vulnerable systems, high-profile targets
  • Motivations: Recognition, curiosity, proving capabilities
  • Methods: Automated scanning, known exploits, DDoS tools

✊ Hacktivists

Politically motivated cyber activists:

  • Resources: Moderate funding, skilled volunteers, coordination
  • Capabilities: DDoS attacks, website defacement, data leaks
  • Targets: Government agencies, corporations, opposing organizations
  • Motivations: Political change, social justice, ideology promotion
  • Examples: Anonymous, LulzSec, Syrian Electronic Army

👤 Insider Threats

Internal organizational risks:

  • Malicious Insiders: Intentional harm, fraud, espionage
  • Negligent Insiders: Accidental exposure, policy violations
  • Compromised Insiders: External control, stolen credentials
  • Risk Factors: Financial stress, job dissatisfaction, ideological conflicts
  • Indicators: Unusual access patterns, data exfiltration, policy violations

💰 Organized Crime

Profit-driven criminal enterprises:

  • Resources: Substantial funding, professional organization
  • Capabilities: Sophisticated malware, money laundering, global reach
  • Targets: Financial institutions, businesses, individual consumers
  • Methods: Ransomware, banking trojans, cryptocurrency theft
  • Structure: Specialized roles, criminal-as-a-Service (CaaS)

👻 Shadow IT

Unauthorized technology deployment:

  • Characteristics: Well-intentioned but risky behavior
  • Examples: Personal cloud storage, unauthorized software
  • Risks: Security control bypass, compliance violations
  • Detection: Network monitoring, endpoint discovery
  • Mitigation: Policy education, approved alternatives

Impact of skill and funding How do threat actor skill and funding level impact the threat to the organization? Skill Level High Skill: Highly skilled attackers, can exploit complex vulnerabilities, bypass security measures, and remain undetected for extended periods. They can target specific systems or individuals within an organization, making them highly dangerous. Low Skill: Unskilled attackers are less likely to launch sophisticated attacks. They may rely on readily available tools or exploit well-known vulnerabilities. However, even unskilled attackers can be dangerous if they target a vulnerable system or trick employees into compromising security (e.g., phishing attacks).

Impact of skill and funding How do threat actor skill and funding level impact the threat to the organization? Funding Level High Funding: Well-funded actors, like nation-states and organized crime groups, can invest in advanced tools, hire skilled attackers, and develop custom malware. This allows them to target a wider range of organizations and launch more complex attacks. Low Funding: Low-funded attackers may rely on free or readily available tools. This can limit their capabilities, but it doesn't eliminate the threat. They can still exploit basic vulnerabilities or launch social engineering attacks that don't require significant resources.
