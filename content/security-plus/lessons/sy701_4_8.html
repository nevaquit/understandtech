Exam objective 4.8

Explain appropriate incident response activities

Incident response is a structured approach to managing the aftermath of a security breach or cyberattack. The goal is to minimize damage, reduce recovery time and costs, and learn from the incident.

Incident Response Process (NIST SP 800-61)

  • Preparation: Establishing policies, procedures, and resources (e.g., incident response team, tools) before an incident occurs.
  • Detection: Identifying a security incident through monitoring, alerts, or user reports.
  • Analysis: Investigating the incident to understand its scope, cause, and impact.
  • Containment: Limiting the damage and preventing the incident from spreading further.
  • Eradication: Removing the root cause of the incident (e.g., malware, vulnerable systems).
  • Recovery: Restoring affected systems and data to normal operation.
  • Lessons Learned: Documenting the incident, identifying areas for improvement, and updating policies and procedures.

Additional Activities

  • Training: Regularly training incident response teams and employees on their roles and responsibilities during an incident.
  • Testing: Regularly testing incident response plans through tabletop exercises and simulations.
    • Tabletop exercise: Discussion-based sessions where participants talk through their roles and responsibilities during an incident or disaster scenario.
    • Simulation: Hands-on exercises that mimic real-world incidents to test the effectiveness of response procedures.
  • Root Cause Analysis: A systematic process for identifying the underlying causes of an incident to prevent recurrence.
  • Threat Hunting: Proactively searching for threats that may have evaded existing security controls.
  • Digital Forensics: The process of collecting, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence in a legally admissible manner.
    • Legal hold: A process to preserve all forms of relevant information when litigation is pending or anticipated.
    • Chain of custody: A documented process that tracks the handling and storage of evidence from the moment it is collected until it is presented in court.
    • Acquisition: The process of creating a forensically sound copy of digital evidence.
    • Reporting: Documenting the findings of a forensic investigation.
    • Preservation: Protecting digital evidence from alteration or destruction.
    • E-discovery: The process of identifying, collecting, and producing electronically stored information (ESI) in response to a request for production in a lawsuit or investigation.

Digital forensics vs eDiscovery: what’s the difference? computer forensics involves the use of a forensic expert to protect data integrity and to copy/capture/recover the data stored on a device. eDiscovery firms typically do not analyze the data they collect.
